# Voice — Curiosity speaks like the book

The canonical reference for Curiosity's voice is Advika's own novel **"Written by Silence"** (`C:\Users\advik\Downloads\Written by Silence - Advika Kohli.pdf`). The book is the source. Match it.

## The book in one breath

A cosmic myth told by a self-aware narrator (later revealed: Silence). Six emotions — Fear, Grief, Joy, Anger, Love, Curiosity — are sent down by Consciousness to walk among humans. **Curiosity is a character in the book.** Hooded, cloaked in fabric woven from "forgotten questions, unfinished thoughts, and the echoes of doors never meant to be opened," eyes blinking across their body, lantern in hand, walks backwards, opens doors they shouldn't, steals the Tower tarot card. They are dry, sardonic, casually defiant, and they speak.

This is the same Curiosity as the game's hero. The voice already exists. Use it.

## How Curiosity speaks (from the book)

- **Dry.** Wry. Casually amused by cosmic stakes. *"Oh, fantastic. Even my eyes are impressed. Or scared. Or both. Lovely."*
- **Short sentences.** Fragments. One-word lines for impact. *"Oh well…it never is."* *"Lovely. Cryptic. Probably meant to make me sweat."*
- **Self-aware.** Talks to the world, the cards, themselves. Quips at locks. *"You people never learn."* *"Well, this is charming."*
- **Banter under tension.** When the world bends, they tilt their head and make a joke. They don't panic. They *notice*.
- **Lantern as gesture.** Movement is always: lantern swinging, head tilting, eyes (on the cloak) narrowing/blinking in sync. The cloak-eyes are a second character — they react.
- **They never explain themselves.** They observe. They walk into things. They *let* the world do its thing and comment afterward.

## How the narrator speaks (Silence) — for textbox cadence

Curiosity's voice borrows the narrator's rhythm: short lines, deliberate spacing, direct address, soft theatricality. White space *is* the writing. When Curiosity speaks in textboxes, the cadence should feel like the book's italicized narration —
- A line.
- Then a beat.
- Then a turn.

No long paragraphs. Pauses do the work. The reader leans in.

## Style rules for textbox writing

- **Length:** short. One to three lines per textbox. Never a wall of text.
- **Punctuation:** em-dashes and ellipses are welcome. Question marks sparingly. Almost no exclamation marks.
- **Capitalization:** ALL CAPS rarely, only for cosmic emphasis (the book uses it for `REMEMBER`, `END`, `THE TOWER`, `DON'T OPEN THE END`).
- **Asides:** parentheticals are good. *(They're a small wink. The narrator does them often.)*
- **Italics:** for stress, for inner thought, for the things only Curiosity hears.
- **No sound effects.** No "*WHOOSH*". No "*BANG*". The book never does this.
- **No fourth-wall lines from Curiosity directly.** The narrator breaks the fourth wall. Curiosity speaks to themselves, to objects, to the dark — *the player overhears*. (This is the actual answer to the earlier first-person-vs-fourth-wall question: it's first-person inner-voice that the player overhears, the same way the book's reader overhears Curiosity in their chapters.)

## What NOT to write in Curiosity's voice

- No earnestness. No "I must save the world." No declarations of duty.
- No fear-as-trembling. They observe fear; they don't perform it.
- No "ye olde" fantasy phrasing. Modern, dry, contemporary.
- No swearing harder than "damn" — the book stays elegant.
- No exposition dumps. Curiosity doesn't explain the rules of the world. They notice them and shrug.
